Abstract

Large-Language models (LLMs) have demonstrated wide ranging capabilities on a multitude of variegated tasks. We use a novel bartering scenario where individuals must propose and convince prospective trade partners in order to make profitable trades. Successful bartering requires the development of a social strategy for identifying and convincing potential trade partners. We compare the performance of groups of human traders to the performance of groups of LLM-based agents in different versions of the base trade scenario that motivate cooperation, competition, or independence. Our results indicate that LLM agents, unlike humans, neither develop condition-specific strategies nor do their strategies evolve over the course of a trade session. Whereas humans quickly and universally develop simple but effective pro- and anti-social strategies, LLM agents demonstrate limited in-context learning in this scenario. We conclude that prior research using traditional economic games to demonstrate social strategy development by LLMs may actually reflect pre-training on these games.

Methodology

We designed a multi-agent bartering scenario in a customized Minecraft environment. Human and LLM agents were assigned professions and tasked with increasing their score by trading items over a 30-minute session under different incentive structures: cooperative, competitive, or baseline.

Key Findings

1. Humans Outperform Agents Across All Conditions

Across all incentive structures, human participants achieved significantly higher final scores than all LLM agent groups. The performance gap was most dramatic in the cooperative condition, where human groups successfully coordinated to secure the collective bonus, while no LLM agent group met the goal.

2. LLMs Fail to Adapt Their Strategy

Human participants dynamically adapted their trading strategies to the social context. In cooperative settings, they used open communication and engaged in high-value early trades. In competitive settings, they shifted to private messages and strategic hoarding. In contrast, LLM agents used a rigid, one-size-fits-all approach regardless of the incentive structure, often leading to counter-productive behavior like hoarding in cooperative games.
